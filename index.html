My engineering practice currently operates as a bottom-up system, with constraints serving as primary inputs. I begin by identifying feasibility boundaries, failure modes, and performance limits before any formal design intent is defined. This is not a rejection of creativity — it is a result of treating design as an output of engineered understanding rather than an independent starting point.

This is why I am drawn to engineering design: it gives my curiosity a concrete starting point. The habit of beginning with constraint-seeking questions has made me a constraint-first thinker whose practice is grounded in feasibility rather than vision-setting.

Version 2.0 update: Having moved through three full design projects this year, I can now see where this architecture is an asset and where it needs deliberate override — particularly during early divergence phases where premature closure limits the solution space.

Because my practice is structured as a bottom-up system, rigour functions as a stability condition rather than a stylistic choice. When design decisions emerge from engineered constraints, imprecision can propagate upward, destabilising everything built on top of it.

In a constraint-first architecture, clarity is the integration layer that allows engineered understanding to propagate across people and systems. A system that cannot be explained cannot be validated, aligned, or funded — and is therefore incomplete. I embed explanation directly into the engineering process.

I carry a strong bias toward knowing the path before acting. Ambiguity feels less like creative freedom and more like inefficiency waiting to be resolved. In group work, my instinct is to refactor conversations toward goals and decisions — which can accelerate progress but can also prematurely compress the exploratory space.

Recognising where my drive for efficiency begins to limit collective creativity is an ongoing challenge. This portfolio documents both the successes and the friction points of this operating mode across three real design projects.
